2024

Ratcliffe-on-Soar power station closes, marking the final use of coal power in Britain.

2018

The Domestic Gas and Electricity Tariff Cap Act 2018 is introduced, insulating consumers from high energy costs.

2010

The Energy Act 2010 is introduced, aiming to accelerate the transition to a low-carbon, high-efficiency economy.

2008

The Energy Act 2008 introduces smart meters and the renewable obligation- supporting the generation of electricity from renewable resources.

2004

The Energy Act 2004 forms the Nuclear Decommissioning Authority, legislating the decommissioning of nuclear generators.

2000

The Utilities Act 2000 establishes the Office of Gas and Electricity Markets (Ofgem)

1990

The Central Electricity Generating Board is privatised.

1989

The Electricity Act 1989 instructs the privatisation of the electricity industry and introduces the fossil fuel levy to support non-fossil fuel-based resources.

1988

Adoption of the European Union's Large Combustion Plant Directive to combat acid rain due to flue gases of power plants.

1986

The Interconnexion France Angleterre (IFA 2000) is commissioned between France and England with a capacity of 2 GW.

1979

Gas turbines used as primary power sources.

1978

The Nuclear Safeguards and Electricity (Finance) Act 1978 implements safeguards in connection with nuclear weapons and provides financing for phase two of Drax power station. Economy 7 tariff introduced for low cost off-peak electricity.

1976

Hinkley Point B and Hunterston B nuclear power stations open using the first advanced gas-cooled reactors.

1974

Drax power station commissions the first 660 MW turbo-alternators.

1973

The North Wales Hydro Electric Power Act 1973 allows the pumped-hydro scheme, Dinorwig, to be constructed.

1972

The Electricity Act 1972 increases the borrowing powers of the Electricity Council and electricity boards in England and Wales.

1969

West Burton A power station opens, becoming the first to have an output of 2 GW.

1966

Ferrybridge C power station becomes the first to use the 500 MW turbo-alternators.

1965

The first phase of the 400 kV super-imposed grid (supergrid) is commissioned between West Burton, Nottinghamshire, and Sundon, Bedfordshire.

1963

Blyth B power station becomes the first to use the 275 MW turbo-alternators.

1962

The first Magnox nuclear power stations are commissioned (Berkeley and Bradwell power stations).

1959

High Marnham power station becomes the largest in Europe with a total capacity of 1 GW. It is the first to use 200 MW tubro-alternators.

1957

The Electricity Act 1957 sets up the Central Electricity Generating Board and Electricity Council to replace the Central Electricity Authority.

1956

Castle Donnington power station becomes the first to use the 100 MW turbo-alternators.

1955

The North Wales Hydro-Electric Power Act 1955 enables the construction of the Ffestiniog and Rheidol hydroelectric schemes.

1953

The first phase of the 275 kV super-imposed grid (supergrid) is commissioned between Staythorpe, Nottinghamshire, and West Melton, South Yorkshire.

1952

The first high voltage direct current link, Interconnexion France Angleterre (IFA 160), is commissioned between France and England with a capacity of 160 MW.

1947

The Electricity Act 1947 combines 625 electricity companies to form twelve area electricity boards for distribution whilst generation and transmission at 132 kV are controlled by the British Electricity Authority.

1933

The 132 kV electricity network becomes interconnected.

1926

The Weir Report and subsequent Electricity (Supply) Act 1926 forms the Central Electricity Board and standardises the electricity transmission system voltage and frequency at 132 kV and 50 Hz, respectively.

1919

The Williamson Report and Birchenough Report leads to the Electricity (Supply) Act 1919 creating the Electricity Commission.

1891

The first phase of the 275 kV super-imposed grid (supergrid) is commissioned between Staythorpe, Nottinghamshire, and West Melton, South Yorkshire.

1882

The Edison Electric Light Station opens as the world's first coal-fired power station at 57 Holborn Viaduct, London, producing 110 volt DC for street lighting. It ran at a loss and closed in 1886.
